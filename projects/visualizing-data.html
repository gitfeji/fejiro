Visualizing Data — Music, Culture & Streaming

What does the music you listen to say about who you are? An information design project examining how age, ethnicity, and streaming habits shape music preferences in the US.

The Premise

Music is deeply personal — but it's also cultural. The genres we gravitate toward, the platforms we stream on, and the artists we consider "representative" of a culture are all shaped by demographic factors we often don't think about consciously.

This project asked: can data reveal the cultural dynamics at play in how Americans consume music? Using three 2018 datasets on genre preferences, streaming platform usage, and ethnicity, I set out to find out.

The Three Hypotheses

Hypothesis 01 — Exploratory

There is a noticeable variation in favourite music genres across different age groups and ethnicities — certain genres will cluster predictably by demographic.

Hypothesis 02 — Explanatory

The form in which music is consumed is influenced by demographic factors like age — streaming platform choice correlates with music preference and age group.

Hypothesis 03 — Predictive

The perception of which music genres "represent modern America" varies significantly across ethnic groups — cultural identity shapes what people see as mainstream.

The Datasets

Three distinct datasets from 2018 formed the foundation of this analysis:

  • Dataset 1 — Favourite music genres broken down by age group, covering Pop, Rock, Hip Hop/Rap, Country, Classical, and more
  • Dataset 2 — Monthly active users across streaming platforms (Spotify, Apple Music, Pandora Radio, etc.), used to infer demographic streaming preferences
  • Dataset 3 — Genre preferences across racial groups, specifically tracking which genres different ethnicities consider representative of modern America

Together, these offered a triangulated view of music consumption — covering what people listen to, where they listen, and what they believe music says about culture.

The Design Challenge

The hardest part of this project wasn't the analysis — it was making three separate datasets feel like one coherent story. Information design at its core is about hierarchy and narrative: what do I show first? What comparison do I want the viewer to make? What should be the emotional takeaway?

I designed a dashboard that moves the viewer through a clear arc — starting with broad genre patterns (what), moving to platform behaviour (how), and landing on cultural perception (why it matters). Each section uses a consistent visual language while allowing each dataset its own visual treatment suited to the type of data.

Key Findings

Finding 01

Pop dominates across younger age groups, while Classic Rock indexes much higher among 45+ listeners — confirming generational genre clustering.

Finding 02

Younger listeners over-index on Spotify and Apple Music, while older demographics skew toward Pandora — platform choice follows age more than genre preference.

Finding 03

Hip Hop/Rap is seen as representative of modern America by a significantly higher proportion of Black respondents than other ethnic groups.

Finding 04

Country music is perceived as "representative of modern America" primarily by white respondents — revealing how cultural identity shapes perception of mainstream.

Reflection

This project expanded my toolkit as a designer. Data visualisation sits at the intersection of research, design, and communication — and getting it right requires all three. I learned that the most important design decisions in data visualisation aren't about chart types or colours — they're about the narrative you choose to tell and the comparisons you invite the viewer to make.

It also made me more sensitive to how data about people can carry cultural weight. The findings here aren't just statistics — they reflect real patterns of identity, belonging, and representation. How you visualise that data, and what you choose to highlight, is itself a design decision with consequences.
