Capstone Project · 2024

Team Canada — Transform Podium

How might digitally-enabled experiences foster safe, inclusive, and barrier-free sport? We investigated that question with real people across 4 countries — and built a mobile app to answer it.

Team Canada (Sheridan College Capstone)
Customer Rep & UX/UI Designer
Morning Owls (5 members)
2024
Interviews · Desk Research · Personas · Prototyping
Final mobile app screens — Home, Explore, Event Detail, and My Events

Overview

This was my Sheridan College capstone project, completed in partnership with Team Canada as part of their Impact Agenda. Our team — the Morning Owls — was given a design challenge rooted in one of Team Canada's three pillars: Podium.

Rather than interpret Podium as elite athletics, we reframed it as a "place" — a digital and physical space where sport becomes accessible to everyone, regardless of background, skill level, or cultural identity. The result was a mobile app that connects community members to inclusive local sports events.

My Role

I wore two hats on this project. As Customer Representative, I was the liaison between our team and Team Canada — gathering requirements, translating feedback, and making sure our work stayed aligned with the partner's expectations.

As Designer, I critically evaluated our team's concepts and contributed to the visual design and prototyping. I was responsible for making sure what we built was both thoughtful and executable.

The Research

Before touching any design tools, we went into the field. We conducted in-depth, one-on-one interviews with 8 participants — 4 within Canada, and 4 internationally (UK, India, South Korea). All were 18+ and active in sports or recreational programs.

We deliberately recruited across gender, age group (Gen Z and Millennials), and cultural background to surface a diverse range of barriers. The conversations were candid, sometimes surprising.

Insight 01

Everyone plays sports — but enjoyment is not guaranteed. Participation doesn't equal inclusion.

Insight 02

Barriers don't discriminate. Cost, language, safety, and social anxiety affect everyone differently.

Insight 03

Inclusivity is not one-size-fits-all. What makes a space safe for one person may exclude another.

Insight 04

There's no such thing as individual sports — community and belonging are always part of the equation.

Insight 05

Canada as a cultural mosaic is a double-edged sword — celebrated diversity can still produce exclusion.

Participant Split

3 male · 5 female · 3 Millennials · 5 Gen Z · 4 countries represented

Personas

After research, we developed two personas to guide design decisions — grounded in real participant patterns, not assumptions.

Arnold Manky
Age 42 · Real estate broker
"I'd love to join a game where everyone has similar skills as me!"
Goals
  • Improve his play skill
  • Find games near his location after work
  • Play with people at his skill level
Pain Points
  • Too busy to call or email centres to find events
  • Worried his kids aren't active enough
Adriana Colure
Age 23 · Masters student at UofT
"If my gym was exclusively for women, I'd up my attendance by 20%!"
Goals
  • Meet like-minded people through sport
  • Find activities in a safe environment
  • Try to genuinely enjoy sports, not just do it for health
Pain Points
  • Worried about unsafe or unwelcoming spaces
  • Doesn't want to call community centres to sign up

The Pivot — Web to Mobile

After initial desk research, we began prototyping a web application. It made sense on paper — broad accessibility, no downloads required. But when we put early screens in front of participants, we hit a wall. The complexity of the features we needed didn't translate well to a web layout.

We went back to our participants with a direct question: web app or mobile app? The answer was unanimous — mobile. So we pivoted. This decision unlocked the features that made the app genuinely useful: QR-based check-in at events, location-based filtering, push notifications for live events, and a more personal, on-the-go experience.

The Solution

We designed a mobile app where community centres and private fitness operators can sign up as event organisers and list sports activities for users to discover and RSVP to. The platform is built around inclusivity filters — women-only events, LGBTQIA+ friendly spaces, skill-level matching (beginner, intermediate, expert), and bilingual support (English and French).

Early web prototype (left) vs final mobile app layouts (right)

Key screens designed:

  • Onboarding — personalised preference setup, sport selection, community type, language choice
  • Home — dashboard with today's events, personalised recommendations, and nearby activities
  • Explore — map and list view with filters for sport type, time, community type, age group, and location radius
  • Event Detail — full event information, skill level indicator, spot availability, and one-tap booking
  • My Events — personal calendar of booked and saved events
  • Profile & Checkout — account management, family member addition, and secure payment flow

Reflection

This project pushed me in ways I didn't expect. Coordinating between a real partner organisation and a five-person team taught me how much of product design is communication — not just pixels. My dual role as customer rep and designer meant I was constantly translating between what the client needed and what users actually wanted.

The pivot from web to mobile was uncomfortable at the time, but it made the product significantly better. That experience reinforced something I now carry into every project: stay curious, stay flexible, and let the research lead.
