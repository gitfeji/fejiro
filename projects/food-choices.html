Food Choices Insights

A qualitative research project exploring how culture, identity, environment, and habit shape the food decisions people make — and what that means for design.

Why Food?

Food is one of the most personal decisions people make every day — and one of the least examined from a UX perspective. It sits at the intersection of culture, identity, health, convenience, memory, and social connection. Understanding why people choose what they eat is really about understanding how people navigate competing needs and values.

This project asked a deceptively simple question: what actually drives the food choices people make?

Research Approach

I conducted in-depth qualitative interviews with participants from diverse cultural and demographic backgrounds. Interviews were open-ended and conversational — designed to surface stories, not just answers. I used research coding (captured in a structured spreadsheet) to identify patterns and themes across responses.

The goal wasn't to reach a statistically significant sample — it was to develop a rich, textured understanding of the forces at play in food decision-making.

Key Themes

Theme 01

Cultural identity — Food is one of the primary ways people express and maintain cultural belonging. What people eat is often tied to who they are.

Theme 02

Convenience vs. values — People consistently compromise their stated food values (health, sustainability) when convenience or time pressure is high.

Theme 03

Social context — Food choices shift dramatically based on who you're eating with. Solo meals and social meals are governed by completely different logic.

Theme 04

Memory and comfort — Many choices trace back to childhood or emotional associations. Familiar foods provide comfort in unfamiliar environments.

Design Implications

This research wasn't tied to a specific product — but the insights point clearly toward how food-related digital products (delivery apps, meal planners, grocery tools) could better serve users:

  • Cultural context should be a first-class feature, not an afterthought — not just cuisine filters, but genuine cultural representation
  • Convenience features should reduce friction without requiring users to compromise their values
  • Social eating modes (ordering for groups, sharing meals) deserve their own interaction patterns
  • Emotional resonance matters — products that acknowledge the memory and comfort dimension of food will feel more human

Reflection

This was one of my first solo research projects, and it taught me how to structure open-ended inquiry. The hardest part wasn't asking questions — it was resisting the urge to lead participants toward conclusions I already suspected. Good research requires genuine curiosity, and this project built that muscle.

It also deepened my appreciation for research as a design skill in its own right. Before I design anything, I want to understand the human context around it — and projects like this one show why that investment pays off.
